Honors Project

This aims of my honors project were to

  1. To evaluate differences in mechanical characteristics during single leg vertical countermovement jump testing, between anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injured limbs and controls during eccentric deceleration and concentric jump phases.
    The metrics evaluated include:
    • Jump height
    • Peak mechanical power
    • Eccentric deceleration impulse
    • Concentric propulsive impusle
    • Eccentric phase duration
    • Groung reaction time
    • Reactive Strength Index = Flight Time Ground Contact Time
  2. To use statistical curve analysis (statistical parametric mapping) for a between-limb analysis of ACL injured athletes and uninjured athletes catagorized into good, fair and poor perception of limb function groups.
  3. To further compare the ACL injured and unjured limbs to the good perception of limb function group.
WHY? Previous liturature has established mechanical deficits in athletes following ACL reconstruction (ACLR), my first aim was to support the existing liturature. After demonstrating these differences, my next step was to explain why these deficits appear after an athlete is cleared to return-to-sport which I did by evaluating the force-time curves of the jumps -> establishing a baseline by which to compare it.

Athlete Monitoring & Data Collection

As a team, we worked directly with University of Calgary athletes to collect pre-season baseline measurements from August-October 2024. Measurements include single and double leg jump testing on force plates as well as maximal isometric and isokinetic strength tests using a robotic leg press dynamometer. In routine athlete monitoring, I lead biweekly in-season jump testing for 4 of the varsity teams. Our routine monitoring helped inform team coaches in training and athletic therapists in return to sport decisons. The database used for the duration of my honors project was comprised of baseline measurements.
